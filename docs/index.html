Most language models write left to right, one token at a time. This one doesn't. It starts from a sequence of pure [MASK] and denoises the whole thing at once — committing the tokens it's surest about first, in whatever order the meaning falls into place.

how it works

Three ideas, no magic

01

Corrupt by masking

The forward process is dead simple: pick a noise level t∈[0,1], then independently replace each token with [MASK] with probability σ(t). At t=1 everything is masked; at t=0 nothing is. No Gaussian noise — text is categorical, so we corrupt by deletion into a single absorbing state.

02

A DiT that reads both ways

A 12-layer Diffusion Transformer (adaLN-Zero conditioning on t) takes the masked sequence and predicts the original token at every position at once. Attention is fully bidirectional — each mask can use context from both sides. That's what a causal LM can't do, and why order stops mattering.

03

Reveal by confidence

Sampling walks t from 1 down to 0. At each step the model scores every masked slot; we commit the most confident ones and freeze them, then re-run with that new context. Confidence is front-loaded where the text is predictable, so the story crystallizes from its anchors outward.

Autoregressive LM

One token at a time, strictly left → right. Position k can never see k+1.

Masked diffusion (this model)

All positions in parallel, surest first. Every token sees the whole sequence.

the build

What it took to make it coherent

The architecture was the easy part. The thing that actually turned word-salad into readable stories was the loss weighting — and it went against the textbook.

The one lever that mattered

The principled ELBO weight w(t)=1/σ(t) down-weights exactly the high-mask regime that generation bootstraps from. Switching to a flat w(t)=1 was a phase change — same model, same data, same steps.

These are samples from a small model trained on a children's-story corpus, so expect simple vocabulary and the occasional slip — what's remarkable is that grammatical, structured stories emerge at all from a process that fills positions out of order.
